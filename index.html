Motivation

I have had many interests relating to engineering since my youth, but the most persistent interest throughout my life has been planetary exploration. In my youth I read many books and watched many documentaries about the exploration of the solar system. I was captivated by the many wonderous worlds of the solar system, and humanity's attempts to visit and learn more about these worlds. The spacecrafts which performed these journeys became personalities to me, with notable personanlities including: Voyager 1 & 2, Cassini, and Opportunity. They have inspired me to pursue a career in aerospace engineering, with my goal for the capstone of my career to work on my own interplanetary mission.

The main thing which drives me is exploration. I am simply curious about how the universe works on a planetary scale, and I want to work on missions which help deepens humanity's knowledge on this topic. The mission concept which interest me the most at present is studying the oceans of icy moons, such as Europa and Enceladus. A quote from an Arthur C. Clarke book comes to mind when I think of this mission concept: "All these worlds are yours, except Europa. Attempt no landings there." I would like to disobey this message and determine whether or not there is life to disturb on Jupiter's icy moon. My secondary motivation is the technical challenge. The reason why I pursued mechanical engineering is because I love designing and building things. Working towards an interplantary mission will only sharpen my skills as a mechanical engineer.
